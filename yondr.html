Yondr makes phone-free spaces for schools, events, and venues. I joined as a mechanical engineer and grew into owning product direction end to end — the kind of scope that normally sits with a Head of Product. What I care about most is the process: how a product moves from a fuzzy need to a made, tested, launched thing — and how you keep everyone aligned the whole way.

How I work: end to end

I own every step of the arc. Here's how a product comes together.

01 — Discovery & requirements

I start with the people. Working with key stakeholders and users, I define the objective, need, and use case — then translate that into critical-to-quality requirements, sorted into must-haves, nice-to-haves, and good-to-haves. Before going further, I check in with leadership, my team, PMs, and the branding team to make sure the priorities are aligned across everyone.

02 — Planning & the product brief

With PMs, marketing, and leadership, I map the full timeline backward from launch — usually a tight few-month window. I fill in milestones, phase durations, key meetings, and production windows, and work with the team on a projected landed cost. Once the brief and CTQs are signed off, I start designing.

03 — Ideation & concepts

I sketch as many ideas as possible, keeping each broad and built around a few big features, and I source competitor and adjacent products for teardown and inspiration. Then I present to the team and leadership, gather feedback, and iterate — combining, adding, and subtracting features across concepts to generate new directions, always keeping everyone in the loop.

04 — Prototyping & evaluation

I move fast into physical prototypes — paper, cardboard, tape, and 3D printing (Bambu Lab) — to pressure-test form and, lightly, function. I build evaluation criteria from my user and stakeholder research, rank the concepts against it, and present the full set alongside the ranked shortlist and my reasoning. A couple more rounds of revision and prototyping narrow it to the strongest one or two.

05 — Vendor sampling & DFMA

I bring manufacturing partners in early — introducing the project, timeline, goals, and cost, and folding their feedback back into the design. For the top concepts I build official tech packs in SolidWorks, Illustrator, and Photoshop, then run 2–3 rounds of sampling, in constant dialogue with vendors on materials, form, function, dimensions, manufacturing methods, assembly, DFMA, and cost — until we land a final iteration that meets every CTQ and the landed-cost target. The core group approves the golden sample.

06 — QA/QC, production & launch

I author the QA/QC protocol covering dimensions, form, function, color, material, and packaging, and coordinate with our lab partner and in-house testing (including quality-assurance and signal-shielding effectiveness). Where the timeline allows, we pilot with a school or venue first, fold in their feedback, get final golden-sample approval, and move into full production and go-to-market — troubleshooting production issues and keeping supply chain, sales, and stakeholders updated throughout.

What I love about this work is that I own the whole arc — and the whole arc is really about people staying aligned. Dozens of iterations, constant cross-functional communication, and a product that has to be right in form, function, and quality before it reaches anyone.

Reflection

The hardest and most rewarding part is holding the tension between a tight timeline and a high bar. Every phase is a negotiation between what's ideal and what's shippable, and doing it well means over-communicating, prototyping early, and bringing manufacturing into the room sooner than feels comfortable. It's taught me that great product development isn't a solo act of design — it's the discipline of keeping many people moving toward the same outcome.
