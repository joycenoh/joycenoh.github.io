Sensus

Sensus was my MIT 2.009 capstone — the class where 18 of us had one theme (Kindness), a $7,000 budget, and three months to take a product from nothing to a working alpha prototype. What we built was Sensus: an at-home screening device that helps people with diabetes catch peripheral neuropathy early, before it becomes something dangerous. I served as co-system integrator, which meant I owned how the whole team and the whole product came together.

Finding the idea

We started wide — individual brainstorms pulling from an idea fair of real problem spaces, then narrowing to six concepts we pitched to the class. Four became sketch models: Boost-It (raising equipment on a ladder), TranquiliSeat (ride stabilization after injury), Blubricator (portable bike-chain lubrication), and PNAT — the earliest version of Sensus. Testing peripheral neuropathy at home, in comfort, kept rising to the top. It was the one that actually helped someone vulnerable.

From concept to architecture

Once Sensus was the direction, we had to turn a good idea into a buildable system. I helped drive the team toward a clear, shared view of the product and its architecture: early-stage CAD models, interaction storyboards mapping every step a user would take, and a full product contract — a description, value proposition, user persona, target market, customer needs, product attributes, and engineering specifications. That contract became our source of truth; when a design question came up, we could point back to what we'd agreed the product actually was.

Our engineering approach was to de-risk early. We tackled the hardest technical challenges up front — testing high-risk aspects physically rather than assuming — so we'd have time to try multiple approaches and still leave room at the end for integration and the inevitable debugging of a full system. We tested often and early, and we made critical design choices as soon as we responsibly could, because every week we bought ourselves at the front was a week of iteration we'd have at the back.

Presenting the work

The process was punctuated by reviews — sketch-model reviews, an assembly review, a technical review, and a final launch-style presentation to the whole class, all the instructors, and guests from real product-development firms. Each was a chance to present a prototype factually to a mixed technical, non-technical, and business audience, take hard feedback, and fold it back in. Learning to stand in front of stakeholders and peers, show the state of the work honestly, and turn critique into the next iteration is a skill I use constantly now.

Designing for a real, vulnerable user

The part I'm proudest of is how seriously we took the human on the other end. We didn't just ask users what they wanted — we observed them, watched one struggle to drive, to manage stairs, to walk, and let what we saw (not what we assumed) drive the specs.

Then something happened in user testing that reshaped the product. Watching an older patient realize their foot couldn't feel anything, we saw them get quietly distressed — worried their neuropathy was racing ahead. That moment pushed us toward a design idea I've held onto ever since.

Emotional design

Because Sensus lived in the medical space, with real patients, we had to consider every interaction someone had with it — not just whether it worked. I leaned on Don Norman's framework of emotional design, which has three branches:

Visceral — sensory input: how things look, feel, and sound.
Behavioral — the pleasure and effectiveness of use: how things function and how usable they are.
Reflective — the meaning of things: self-image, personal satisfaction, and memory.

Mapping those onto Sensus shaped real decisions. Visceral: we gave it a sleek, non-intrusive aesthetic that blended into a home, so it wouldn't sit there like a medical device constantly reminding someone of their condition. Behavioral: testing with an older patient taught us to make the process effortless — stay seated, lift your feet just a couple of inches onto the surface, done. Reflective: after seeing that patient's distress, we made sure the app framed everything gently — a personal check to help you decide whether to see a doctor soon, explicitly never a diagnosis. Designing around someone's fear, not just their symptom, is the lesson from Sensus I carry into everything.

We changed the way we thought — from focusing on the solution to focusing on the problem, and on the person living inside it.

The final presentation

The final milestone was a formal, new-product-launch-style presentation to the entire class, all the instructors, and guests from product-development firms — a few minutes to demo the alpha prototype and present the key needs, technical innovation, and business case, followed by live Q&A. Reflection

Sensus made me a better leader, engineer, and teammate all at once. It taught me to delegate to people's strengths, to keep communication fluid enough that no one felt lost, and to hold a master timeline everyone could fall back on. But the deepest lesson was about openness — that there's never one right way into a problem, and the best work comes from staying curious, exploring past your comfort zone, and keeping the person you're designing for at the center. The team won the Thomas Sheridan Award for creativity in human-machine integration, but what I actually walked away with was a way of working I still use.
