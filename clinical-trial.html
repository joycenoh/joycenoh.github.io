This was my MEng thesis, done with MIT and Tufts Medical Center. Remote clinical trials were rapidly changing how studies get run — reaching people who can't come in for hospitalization, or who live too far from the nearest hospital — but there was no standardized framework for how to actually operate them. My team set out to build one. My piece was the physical kit: a "clinical trial in a box" shipped to a patient's home that collects their physiological data safely and confidentially, so they can take part in a study without ever setting foot in a clinic.

The mission is what drew me in — it's about access. A well-designed box is the difference between a trial that only reaches people near big academic hospitals and one that reaches everyone else.

The framework

Our team designed a ten-step framework for standing up remote clinical trial operations — covering trial parameters, participant tracking, device storage and cleaning, inventory systems, box design, assembly, and delivery/returns. My thesis owned box design: the packaging that holds everything and guides the patient through the trial. (My collaborators Carly Smith, Imane Ait Mbiriq, and Ryan Lin covered patient education, supply chain, and assembly process.)

Research: learning from what exists

Before designing anything, I studied packaging that already solved related problems — Apple's product packaging, Pelican cases, spring-loaded glasses cases, at-home clinical kits, even a zippered backgammon set. I evaluated each against six questions: what makes it unique, is it visually appealing, what does it hold, is it easy to use, what materials does it use and are they sustainable, and is it durable — then built advantage/disadvantage tables to carry the best ideas forward.

Defining the requirements

Working from the Tufts team's input and the patient's perspective, I set the design requirements and ranked them by importance. The box had to do a lot at once: appeal across age demographics (a child and an adult might both receive one), integrate into a patient's home while still reading as important enough not to toss, keep contents secure through shipping and use, make it obvious what to do the moment it's opened, clearly mark which items need to be returned and how, open and close effortlessly, and use materials durable and sustainable enough to be reused or recycled.

Box contents

The contents change per trial — this project focused on two, the niclosamide trial and the long-COVID trial. For every item I catalogued its description, dimensions, and whether it had to be returned at the end, so the packaging could be designed around exactly what it needed to hold.

CAD & the design

I modeled the box in Autodesk Fusion — one universal 23 × 19 × 13" hard case sized to fit the contents of both trials, with custom foam inserts holding each device in place. The design took its cues from a Pelican case: a polypropylene hard shell durable enough for shipping, handling, and life in someone's home; foam inserts that secure every component and make it obvious where each one goes; and color-coded, labeled cutouts so a patient can instantly see which items to return and exactly how to repack them. Both the shell and the inserts are reusable across future trials.

Manufacturing & returns

I planned the whole thing to be made and reused, not thrown away. The universal plastic parts — lid, body, handle, hinge, hinge pins, latch — are injection molded, so tooling cost is offset the more boxes you make; the foam inserts are cut on a computer-controlled waterjet. Because the boxes are reusable, you only manufacture to demand and lifetime. Every kit ships with instructions for the trial and for returns, a return shipping label, and those color-coded foam cutouts guiding what goes back and where.

The whole design question was really one question: how do you make a stranger, alone at home, feel confident doing something a clinician would normally do for them?

Reflection

This project is the most complete arc of engineering I've owned end to end — precedent research, ranked requirements, CAD, material selection, and a manufacturing-and-returns plan, all in service of a real clinical partner and real patients. It taught me that thoughtful packaging isn't an afterthought; for a remote patient, the box is the experience, and getting it right is what lets someone participate in their own care from home.
