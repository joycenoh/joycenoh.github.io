Flexural Vane Pump

Redesigning a rotary vane pump with flexures — with potential for desalination

Year
2022
Discipline
Engineering
Role
Mechanical Design Engineer
Tools
Fusion 360, FDM printing
Lab
MIT GEAR Lab

This project came out of the MIT GEAR Lab, which builds high-performance, low-cost technology for emerging markets. The target application: desalination — turning seawater into drinkable water, cheaply enough to matter where it's needed most.

A rotary vane pump moves fluid using vanes that slide in and out of a spinning rotor while pressing against the surrounding stator wall. That contact is everything — any gap leaks and the pump stops working. But the usual way of maintaining it, through vane geometry and load, creates friction and can cause the vanes to jam. My question was simple: what if the vane could keep contact with the wall by bending instead of scraping? That's the idea behind a flexural vane — using elastic flexures to hold contact while designing the friction problem away.

Generating designs

I explored four flexure concepts, each a different way to get clean linear motion out of a bending element. Design 1 used eight flexure strips in a single chamber — the most robust motion and the least racking, but the most complex to build. Design 2 simplified to four strips across two chambers — easier to make, but with less range and more stress on each strip. Design 3 used two flexure lengths between fixed and free blocks to unlock a large, well-constrained range of motion. Design 4 took a different path entirely: coils anchoring the vane in two chambers, giving stable motion and steady wall contact without over-forcing it.

Choosing a direction

I judged the concepts against four functional requirements: the vane has to keep contact with the stator wall, minimize friction on both the channel and the wall, survive the stresses without failing, and stay reasonably simple to make. I picked one design per flexure type to carry forward — Design 3 (strips) and Design 4 (coils) — so I could compare the two fundamentally different approaches head to head.

CAD & prototyping

I modeled the chosen designs in Fusion 360 and 3D-printed them on a Stratasys Fortus (FDM). Prototyping is where theory meets reality, and reality had opinions.

What the prototype taught me

The honest results were the most useful part. The 3D-printed plastic flexure strips didn't behave like the thin metal ones the concept assumed — under very little strain, they snapped, limiting the vane's motion. Printing the vane as one assembly with its flexures also meant it couldn't actually be fitted into the rotor. But there were good surprises too: the full assembly printed stronger and closer to spec than expected, and the coils came out far less stiff than predicted — which, happily, gave the vane a greater range of motion. The clear next iterations: switch the flexures to thin sheet metal, and print the vane as separate parts that assemble into the rotor.

Until you test it, you don't have a solution — you have a hypothesis. The prototype is where you find out which one you've got.

Reflection

A few lessons stuck with me from this one. First, on communication: I met weekly with my graduate supervisor but kept an open channel for the in-between moments, and I learned that asking questions isn't weakness — it's boldness. It means you care enough to do the work right.

Second, on research: it's the unglamorous foundation of everything, and it pays off most when you chase it with real curiosity — I found flexure ideas everywhere from papers to random YouTube videos of the simplest linear mechanisms. And on ideation, the mantra I keep: brainstorm like a storm — wild, spread out, boundless. My best ideas rarely come from staring at the problem; they come from noticing the mundane in daily life, from sports and fashion and nature, from things that seemed unrelated until suddenly they weren't.

And finally, testing. You can do all the theory, modeling, and research in the world, but until you put a design in a machine or in someone's hands, you don't know. Test often, test early — it's the only way to find out what to fix.
